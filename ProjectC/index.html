Food insecurity grows in New York City's post-pandemic landscape

By Lara Heard

20 months have passed since New York first went into lockdown. But New Yorkers are still suffering the effects of the pandemic, including heightened rates of food insecurity.

According to data from the Human Resources Administration, food pantry usage increased by about 94% between the first and second quarters of 2020. That trend has continued into 2021; between April and June, usage rates were 144% higher than during the same months of 2019.

The effects of the pandemic on New Yorkers' food security came swiftly. In May 2021, according to NPR, Mayor de Blasio announced at a daily briefing that the number of food insecure individuals was estimated to have doubled from about 1 million to 2 million following the pandemic. Food Bank For NYC attributes this rise to a combination of multiple conditions, each conducive to food insecurity, including high unemployment and inflated food prices. Food insecurity disproportionately impacts New Yorkers of color.

This increase comes despite measures taken during the pandemic to improve food access. According to a CUNY Graduate School of Public Health & Health Policy survey, New Yorkers' usage of SNAP benefits greatly increased between April and September. Many respondents were also taking advantage of the city schools' Grab and Go Food program.

But providing food access to New Yorkers will require more than just a reversal of pandemic era harm. In a written testimony regarding hunger in New York City, Charles Platkin, Executive Director of the Hunter College New York City Food Policy Center, wrote that "Our aim cannot be to return to the pre-pandemic normal, as that would still leave approximately 1.2 million people hungry and food insecure."

Instead, Platkin called for "long-term solutions to ensure that all New Yorkers have access to healthy, nutritious meals at home, at school, and at work."
